The Man

Lt. Colonel
John Ira Mulzac, Sr.

1923
Born, Baltimore — Raised Brooklyn
Born at Johns Hopkins Hospital in Baltimore, Maryland. Brought to Brooklyn as a young child — raised and schooled there. Left high school at 17 to support his family, working as a radio technician at Fort Monmouth, NJ.
1942–1944
Tuskegee · Class 44-J
Enlisted at 18. Assigned to the 99th Fighter Squadron, then 332nd Fighter Group and 477th Bombardment Group. Earned his wings from Tuskegee Army Air Field — a graduate without a high school diploma.
WWII · Korea · Vietnam
Three Wars. One Pilot.
Flew the P-38 Lightning, B-25 Mitchell, C-119 Flying Boxcar, and C-97 Stratocruiser. Flew missions across three decades of American military service.
1947–1967
FDNY Lieutenant
Served 20 years with the New York City Fire Department, rising to Lieutenant. Activated twice for military duty during his FDNY tenure.
1971–1983
Sky Marshal · U.S. Customs
Among the first U.S. Federal Sky Marshals. Then 21 years as a U.S. Customs Inspector at JFK. Three separate careers. Three pensions.
2007
Congressional Gold Medal
Honored at the U.S. Capitol by President George W. Bush alongside nearly 300 Tuskegee Airmen or their surviving widows.
"Where else could I have become a lieutenant colonel flying airplanes without even finishing high school?" — Lt. Col. John Mulzac, U.S. Air Force Veterans in Blue
Family Legacy

8 children — all college graduates. 4 daughters earned master's degrees. Son Robert became an FDNY Lieutenant. Grandson Tobias Harris currently plays for the Detroit Pistons — a city with its own chapter in his grandfather's story (see slide 5). A granddaughter was accepted to the Naval Academy. His wife Beatrice pinned on his wings at graduation in 1944 — and was by his side for 55 years.

The Watch

The A-11 Hack
The Watch That Won The War

Bulova was one of three original manufacturers contracted to build the A-11 — the standard field watch issued to Allied pilots during WWII. Its hack feature allowed synchronized precision. The same precision that Tuskegee Airmen brought to every mission.

⏱
The Feature
The Hack
Pilots pulled the crown to stop the seconds hand and synchronize watches before a mission. A small mechanical act that coordinated attacks with split-second timing. It was new technology in 1942 — and it was on every pilot's wrist.
✈
The Context
Issued to Pilots
The A-11 was primarily issued to aviators — the same role John Mulzac trained for at Tuskegee. The watch and the pilot were born in the same moment in American history. This edition closes that loop.
🗽
The Heritage
New York to New York
Bulova: founded in New York City, 1875, still headquartered there. John Mulzac: born Brooklyn, raised Brooklyn, served Brooklyn. This is a genuinely New York watch honoring a genuinely New York hero — not a borrowed association.

01
The Convergence
Two New York institutions — one a watch company on Fifth Avenue, one a teenager from Bed-Stuy — both answered America's call in the same year. Bulova produced the A-11 for the pilots of WWII. John Mulzac became one of those pilots. The watch that was made for men like him is now being made in his honor. That's not marketing. That's history closing a circle.
Authenticity · Heritage · New York
02
The Hack as Metaphor
The "hack" feature — stopping time to synchronize with precision before launching a mission — is an extraordinary metaphor for what the Tuskegee Airmen did. Asked to prove their worth in a system designed to exclude them, they responded with discipline, precision, and an unbroken record of excellence. John Mulzac refused to bail out of a disabled aircraft at 2,000 feet. He brought it home. That's the hack watch embodied.
Storytelling · Mission · Excellence
03
The Detroit Heist
This is the story that should be on the caseback.

John Mulzac was stationed at Selfridge Field, Michigan — assigned to the 332nd Fighter Group as a radio technician, the top man in his unit, being prepared for overseas deployment. He wanted to fly. But the official path to pilot training required college credits he didn't have, and the Air Force system wasn't going to give him a way in.

One Saturday, a friend was driving a truck into Detroit to pick up provisions for the base. Mulzac asked to ride along — didn't say why. His friend dropped him at the civilian induction center: the place where men came to enlist, not where serving soldiers applied for anything. Inside, two sergeants. A white sergeant told him no. A Black sergeant said: "Go ahead, let him take the test. What difference does it make?"

He took the test. His friend came back. They returned to base.

Three weeks later, his commanding officer — Captain Tresville, West Point graduate — called him in. A letter had arrived ordering Mulzac to Biloxi, Mississippi to begin pilot training. Tresville tried to stop him: "I have a good mind not to let you go. You're my top man." Mulzac told him: "Sir, I always want to be like you. I want to fly."

Tresville let him go. Then he picked up the phone and called Detroit — and closed the loophole for everyone else. Mulzac's buddies found out and wanted to do the same. They were already too late.

Captain Tresville was later killed in Italy. The advice he gave Mulzac as he left — "there are old pilots and bold pilots, but no old, bold pilots" — Mulzac carried for thirty years of flying.

That act of quiet determination in a Detroit induction center, on a borrowed Saturday, is what made John Mulzac a pilot. His grandson Tobias Harris now plays for the Detroit Pistons — currently in his second stint in the city. The place that launched one legacy now hosts another.
Detroit · Selfridge Field · Defiance · Full Circle · Tobias Harris
04
The Oral History Asset
A full 40-page oral history transcript exists — recorded for the National Park Service's Tuskegee Airmen Oral History Project in 2001. Stories of nearly bailing out over Alabama, meeting Dr. George Washington Carver, running a laundry business as a cadet, being roommates with "Chappie" James. These are cinematic, specific, human stories with primary-source documentation. Content that cannot be manufactured.

Black nylon NATO strap with red edge stitching — a subtle nod to the Red Tails of the 332nd Fighter Group, of which Mulzac was part before the 477th. The red seconds hand and red stitching create visual coherence across the complete timepiece.
Edition Size
477 or 992 Pieces
Number the edition to 477 — honoring the 477th Bombardment Group — or 992, the total number of pilots trained at Tuskegee Army Air Field. Each option carries a specific, verifiable historical weight. Numbered individually on the caseback.
RECOMMENDATION
477 pieces creates higher scarcity and collector urgency. 992 tells the broader Tuskegee story. Consider 477 for a premiere run with a subsequent 992-piece general edition.
